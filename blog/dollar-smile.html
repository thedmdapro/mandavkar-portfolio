The dollar smile and why EM portfolios should watch US data first.

The dollar strengthens both when the US economy booms and when global risk-off hits. This U-shaped relationship — the dollar smile — is the single most useful macro framework for anyone managing EM exposure.

Dhruv Mandavkar Coming soon ~6 min read

The "dollar smile" is a framework developed by Stephen Jen, former Morgan Stanley strategist, to describe the non-linear relationship between US economic conditions and the USD. The theory: the dollar tends to be strong at both extremes — when the US economy is performing exceptionally well relative to the rest of the world, and when global risk sentiment collapses. The dollar is weakest in the middle, when US growth is moderate and global risk appetite is healthy.

This creates a U-shaped curve: plot US growth relative to global growth on the x-axis, and USD strength on the y-axis. The curve smiles. The practical implication for EM investors is significant — there are two distinct dollar-strengthening regimes, and they require different EM portfolio responses. A dollar rally driven by US outperformance (right side of the smile) typically allows selective EM countries with strong fundamentals to hold up. A dollar rally driven by global risk-off (left side of the smile) hits everything in EM simultaneously and indiscriminately.

Knowing which part of the smile you are in determines which EM assets are safe to hold through dollar strength and which ones are not. The signals for distinguishing the two regimes are observable — credit spreads, commodity prices, EM vs DM equity performance, and the velocity of the dollar move all tell you something about the mechanism.
