What DCF doesn't capture (and what to do about it).

DCF is the canonical valuation tool — precise, structured, internally consistent. It is also systematically wrong in the same ways every market cycle. Understanding where the model fails is where the edge actually is.

Dhruv Mandavkar Coming soon ~8 min read

Every CFA curriculum, every investment banking training programme, and every equity research team in the world teaches DCF as the foundational valuation framework. Discount the cash flows. Calculate the terminal value. Arrive at a fair value. Compare to the current price. Act accordingly.

The framework is internally consistent and theoretically sound. The problem is that its blind spots are structural, not random. DCF systematically overvalues companies during rate-compression cycles (because analysts anchor growth assumptions to recent elevated performance and use low discount rates simultaneously). It systematically undervalues capital-light businesses with strong network effects (because the terminal value understates the compounding capacity of these businesses over 10+ year horizons). And it is nearly useless for valuing companies where the value creation mechanism is non-linear — platform businesses, businesses in early-stage scaling, or companies where regulatory or competitive moat changes are the key variable.

The practitioners who use DCF well know this. They treat the model not as an answer-generating machine but as a structured way to think about the key assumptions, stress-test them, and understand what the market is implicitly pricing. A DCF backwards-solved from the current stock price — "what does the market believe the terminal growth rate is?" — is often more useful than a forward DCF built from first principles.
