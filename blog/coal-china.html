China · Commodities · Macro Methodology

I track coal imports before calling China's industrial recovery.

China's official PMI data is available but often distrusted by the market. Coal import volumes at key ports are a harder, faster, and less-managed signal of what is actually happening in Chinese industry.

Dhruv Mandavkar Coming soon ~6 min read

The most common question in global commodity markets over the past three years has been some variant of: "Is China actually recovering?" The question keeps arising because official data — GDP growth, PMI surveys, industrial production figures — has been difficult to reconcile with what is visible in other datasets. Satellite imagery of factory activity, freight volumes, property construction activity, and commodity import data have all at various points diverged from the headline numbers.

My preferred cross-check is thermal coal import volumes at Qinhuangdao, Caofeidian, and the major southern ports. China is the world's largest producer of coal domestically, but it also imports substantial quantities when domestic supply falls short of demand. The import number is difficult to manage at the level of granularity that GDP or PMI figures can be. It is a physical trade flow that requires actual vessels, actual cargoes, and actual port throughput. It shows up in customs data, shipping intelligence, and port authority releases — multiple independent sources that are hard to coordinate if someone wanted to present a misleading picture.

The logic is simple: thermal coal powers the steel mills, cement plants, aluminum smelters, and chemical facilities that constitute China's industrial base. When those plants are running at high utilisation, coal demand rises. When coal inventories at ports are falling despite elevated imports, it means consumption is outpacing supply. That is a genuine industrial activity signal.
