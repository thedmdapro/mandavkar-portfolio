About
01
Origin

I grew up across Saudi Arabia, South Korea, China, and India. Four completely different relationships with money, risk, saving, and markets. What that taught me — and what no textbook did — is that financial behaviour doesn't generalise across borders. The assumptions embedded in most Western financial models were built for Western financial psychology. The rest of the world doesn't always work that way, and the gaps between those assumptions and reality are where the most interesting signals live.

02
Lens

That shapes how I approach research. Before I open an earnings report, I check what's happening upstream — Baltic Dry, LNG spot, commodity flows, shipping route disruptions. My starting assumption is that the physical economy moves before the financial one acknowledges it. Red Sea rerouting, Hormuz risk, rare earth processing concentration — I track these because I think the pricing lag between physical disruption and financial adjustment is where the most interesting research questions live.

03
Method

Everything I publish follows the same structure: start with a physical or macro signal, verify it against multiple data sources, then trace the implications down to sectors and securities. I built a live dashboard that pulls 21 indicators from FRED on every page load. I wrote 12 structural theses connecting supply chain disruptions to specific asset classes. The point isn't prediction — it's building a research process that surfaces questions worth investigating before they become consensus. That's what I want to do professionally, and London is where the work is.

04
Maritime

I have a particular focus on maritime trade and shipping economics — the physical infrastructure that most financial models treat as a given. Chinese yards now build more than half the world's ships, operate the world's largest merchant fleet, and have embedded military conversion requirements into civilian vessels by law since 2016. Beidou tracking covers the entire Chinese fishing and merchant fleet in real time while Chinese waters have gone dark to global AIS monitoring since 2021. These are not defence industry stories. They are freight market stories, energy security stories, and capital allocation stories — and they connect directly to the macro signals I track weekly.

Where I Stand

Five positions I'll defend.

Opinions, not predictions. I'm willing to be wrong.

01 Commodities
"Physical signals are the only macro data that can't be managed."
The evidence

The Baltic Dry Index doesn't have an investor relations team. Corn doesn't have forward guidance. LNG spot prices don't get revised three months later. When physical commodity signals diverge from equity markets or central bank narratives, I trust the ships, the grain, and the gas. One of them is reflecting reality — and it's never the press release.

02 India
"India's retail investment boom is structurally underestimated."
The evidence

India went from 40 million to 216 million demat accounts in six years. Global analysts model this as a cyclical trend. It isn't. It's a structural shift driven by smartphones, UPI infrastructure, and a generation that trusts markets over bank deposits. The wealth management industry hasn't priced the scale of what's coming — and the monthly SIP data is the cleanest signal of whether it continues.

03 Energy
"Nuclear energy is the most mispriced asset class in the world right now."
The evidence

AI data centres are repricing power demand faster than any grid infrastructure can respond. A single large language model training run consumes as much electricity as a small city for weeks. Uranium supply is structurally constrained — mine output hasn't kept pace with reactor restarts, and enrichment capacity is concentrated in Russia and China. New reactor builds take a decade. The companies that own operating nuclear capacity today are sitting on assets that the market valued for a low-growth utility world, not for an AI-driven power demand supercycle. The repricing has started but is nowhere near complete.

04 Geopolitics
"Geopolitical realignment creates pricing dislocations that DCF can't see."
The evidence

The Hormuz closure threatens one-third of global helium supply and 20% of LNG simultaneously. The Russia-Ukraine war rewired European energy infrastructure permanently. Standard valuation models don't price geopolitical risk — they can't, because the inputs don't appear in earnings forecasts or discount rates. Which means every dislocation it creates is, for a brief window, mispriced. That window is where the trade is.

05 Market Microstructure
"Crypto regulation creates more volatility than it suppresses — at least in the short run."
The evidence

This isn't a political position. It's an empirical finding. In my research, regulatory events stabilised traditional securities (S&P 500 barely moved) while consistently amplifying crypto volatility — ETH showed Wilcoxon significance at p=0.0284 in the long window. The mechanism is speculative trading: regulatory announcements compress or expand participation expectations, triggering price discovery in a market with no institutional stabiliser equivalent. MiCA may change this over a 5–10 year horizon as the institutional base deepens. But right now, the data says regulation and crypto volatility move together, not against each other.

Thinking About

What happens when the yen carry trade fully unwinds? BOJ rate normalisation is unwinding the largest carry trade in history. If USDJPY breaks below 140, the forced deleveraging could cascade through every risk asset class simultaneously. Where I lean: most portfolios have zero hedging for this. The asymmetry favours owning volatility — the unwind will be non-linear and the correlation spike across asset classes will be unlike anything since March 2020.

Can the green transition happen at current copper prices? Electrification, grid buildout, and data centre cooling all need copper, but mining capex peaked a decade ago and new projects take 10+ years from discovery to production. Where I lean: no. The transition requires prices significantly above spot to incentivise new supply. That means either copper reprices upward or the transition timeline slips. Either way, copper miners with permitted reserves are structurally undervalued.

Who absorbs the margin compression from Chinese overcapacity? China is exporting deflation. Industrial overcapacity in EVs, solar, and steel is dumping cheap goods globally. Tariffs slow it but don't stop it. Where I lean: European industrial companies are most exposed — especially chemicals and basic materials where energy costs already compress margins. Indian manufacturers have partial tariff protection but are still vulnerable on inputs. Watch BASF, Thyssenkrupp, and Tata Steel Europe as leading indicators.

Who controls the uranium enrichment bottleneck? Even if the West restarts nuclear capacity at scale, enrichment is the chokepoint. Russia's Rosatom controls ~40% of global enrichment. The US and Europe are trying to build domestic capacity, but centrifuge facilities take years. Where I lean: enrichment is the real constraint, not reactor builds or even uranium mining. The companies and countries that secure enrichment capacity first will set the terms for the entire nuclear renaissance. This is an energy security story disguised as a utility investment story.
