About

Macro analyst. I track the signals that can't be managed.

I grew up across Saudi Arabia, South Korea, China, and India. Four completely different relationships with money, risk, saving, and markets. What that taught me — and what no textbook did — is that financial behaviour doesn't generalise across borders. The assumptions embedded in most Western financial models were built for Western financial psychology. The rest of the world doesn't always work that way, and the gaps between those assumptions and reality are where the most interesting signals live.

That's the lens I bring to markets. Before I look at a company's earnings, I check what's happening upstream — Baltic Dry, LNG spot, commodity flows, shipping route disruptions. The physical economy moves before the financial one acknowledges it. Disruptions that are happening right now in the Strait of Hormuz, in Red Sea shipping lanes, in rare earth processing — these have consequences that will take years to fully price in. Most market participants are still treating them as temporary noise. I don't think they are.

The deeper version of this is pattern recognition at scale. The most actionable signals in macro aren't the obvious ones — they're the coordinated movements of large institutional capital that haven't yet surfaced in public data. Sovereign wealth fund deployment. Central bank reserve reallocation. The quiet accumulation that precedes a thesis becoming consensus. I'm interested in learning to read those patterns early. That's ultimately why I want to be in London, at the intersection of global capital flows, building toward running my own macro strategy.

I have a particular focus on maritime trade and shipping economics — the physical infrastructure that most financial models treat as a given. Chinese yards now build more than half the world's ships, operate the world's largest merchant fleet, and have embedded military conversion requirements into civilian vessels by law since 2016. Beidou tracking covers the entire Chinese fishing and merchant fleet in real time while Chinese waters have gone dark to global AIS monitoring since 2021. These are not defence industry stories. They are freight market stories, energy security stories, and capital allocation stories — and they connect directly to the macro signals I track weekly. I am building systematic knowledge of shipping economics, vessel markets, and maritime geopolitics alongside my macro research.

Where I Stand

Five positions I'll defend.

Opinions, not predictions. I'm willing to be wrong.

01 Commodities
"Physical signals are the only macro data that can't be managed."
The evidence

The Baltic Dry Index doesn't have an investor relations team. Corn doesn't have forward guidance. LNG spot prices don't get revised three months later. When physical commodity signals diverge from equity markets or central bank narratives, I trust the ships, the grain, and the gas. One of them is reflecting reality — and it's never the press release.

02 India
"India's retail investment boom is structurally underestimated."
The evidence

India went from 40 million to 216 million demat accounts in six years. Global analysts model this as a cyclical trend. It isn't. It's a structural shift driven by smartphones, UPI infrastructure, and a generation that trusts markets over bank deposits. The wealth management industry hasn't priced the scale of what's coming — and the monthly SIP data is the cleanest signal of whether it continues.

03 Dissertation Research
"Social media sentiment is a macro signal that most institutional models ignore at their peril."
The evidence

In my dissertation research, Reddit negative sentiment Granger-caused crypto volatility with Chi² statistics above 27 at a single lag — stronger predictive power than most traditional macro variables produce in comparable tests. The mechanism isn't irrational retail noise. It's sentiment contagion: negative narratives spreading through retail networks amplify selling pressure before it registers in price data. Any volatility model that doesn't account for sentiment is working with incomplete information. This applies beyond crypto — the transmission mechanism is the same wherever retail participation is high.

04 Geopolitics
"Geopolitical realignment creates pricing dislocations that DCF can't see."
The evidence

The Hormuz closure threatens one-third of global helium supply and 20% of LNG simultaneously. The Russia-Ukraine war rewired European energy infrastructure permanently. Standard valuation models don't price geopolitical risk — they can't, because the inputs don't appear in earnings forecasts or discount rates. Which means every dislocation it creates is, for a brief window, mispriced. That window is where the trade is.

05 Crypto
"Crypto regulation creates more volatility than it suppresses — at least in the short run."
The evidence

This isn't a political position. It's an empirical finding. In my research, regulatory events stabilised traditional securities (S&P 500 barely moved) while consistently amplifying crypto volatility — ETH showed Wilcoxon significance at p=0.0284 in the long window. The mechanism is speculative trading: regulatory announcements compress or expand participation expectations, triggering price discovery in a market with no institutional stabiliser equivalent. MiCA may change this over a 5–10 year horizon as the institutional base deepens. But right now, the data says regulation and crypto volatility move together, not against each other.

Thinking About

What happens when the yen carry trade fully unwinds? BOJ rate normalisation is unwinding the largest carry trade in history. If USDJPY breaks below 140, the forced deleveraging could cascade through every risk asset class simultaneously — and most portfolios have zero hedging for it.

Is India's MSCI EM weight a structural bid or a crowding trap? India is approaching 20% of the index. Every basis point increase triggers passive inflows that have nothing to do with fundamentals. The structural bid is becoming self-reinforcing — and the crowding risk when it reverses is enormous.

Can the green transition happen at current copper prices? Copper supply is structurally short. Electrification, grid buildout, and data centre cooling all need it, but mining capex peaked a decade ago. The transition can't happen without a price high enough to justify new mines — and that price is significantly above spot.

Is the yield curve still a recession signal, or a fiscal one? US Treasury issuance is crowding out private credit. When the government is borrowing $2T+ annually, every other borrower pays more. The fiscal dominance regime means the curve is no longer just signalling recession — it's signalling fiscal sustainability.

Who absorbs the margin compression from Chinese overcapacity? China is exporting deflation. Industrial overcapacity in EVs, solar, and steel is dumping cheap goods globally. Tariffs slow it but don't stop it. The margin pressure on European and Indian manufacturers is only starting.

Is nuclear the most mispriced energy asset in the world right now? AI data centre power demand is repricing nuclear energy. Uranium supply is constrained, enrichment capacity is concentrated in Russia, and new reactor builds take a decade. The companies that own operating nuclear capacity today have an asset that's about to be repriced.

What does a $100+ Brent spike do to Indian equity multiples? India's current account is structurally hostage to crude. Every $10/bbl move costs ~$15B annually. If oil spikes on a supply disruption, the RBI burns reserves, the rupee weakens, and equity multiples compress — all at once. It's the single biggest macro risk to Indian equity positioning.

Is gold at $3,000 expensive, or is it being repriced? Central banks bought more gold in 2023-2025 than any period since Bretton Woods. The signal isn't inflation hedging — it's de-dollarisation. If the trend holds, gold isn't expensive. It's being repriced structurally as a reserve asset.
