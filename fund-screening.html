Screening Framework

A 3-layer systematic screening model covering 80 Indian equity mutual funds across 4 SEBI categories. Pass/fail gates, 5-factor quantitative scoring, and qualitative analyst overlay. Built from ACE MF data sourced directly from AMC filings.

Context

Why Build a Screening Model

India's mutual fund industry has ~₹67 lakh crore in equity AUM across hundreds of schemes. Most retail investors pick funds based on trailing 1-year returns or star ratings from aggregators. The problem: trailing returns are backward-looking, and star ratings collapse multi-dimensional fund quality into a single number with opaque methodology.

This model was built during my time at Omega Portfolio Advisors / MoneyWorks4Me to power fund recommendations for Third Rock Wealth's (TRW) client-facing dossiers. The goal was a repeatable, auditable framework that separates genuine alpha generators from funds riding beta in a rising market.

The core design choice: score funds within their own SEBI category using percentile ranks, not absolute values. A large-cap fund generating 1.5% alpha is doing something meaningfully different from a small-cap fund generating 1.5% alpha. The benchmark difficulty, volatility profile, and capacity constraints are entirely different. Category-relative scoring is the only honest comparison.

Framework

Three Layers of Filtration

Every fund in the starting universe passes through three sequential layers. Each layer eliminates or scores, so that the final output is a ranked shortlist with both quantitative rigour and qualitative context.

Layer 1

Pass / Fail Gates

Binary filters that eliminate funds with structural disqualifiers. Funds must pass all gates to enter the scoring engine. The gates remove funds that are too small, too young, or too expensive to be taken seriously for long-term allocation.

AUM Floor ≥ ₹500 Cr
Fund Age ≥ 5 Years
Expense Ratio ≤ 1.0% (Direct)
Track Record 5Y CAGR available
Result 133 → 80 funds passed
Layer 2

5-Factor Quantitative Scoring

Each surviving fund is scored on 5 weighted factors, producing a composite score from 0 to 100. All sub-metrics are converted to within-category percentile ranks before weighting, so the scoring is relative to peers in the same SEBI category.

Factor Weight Key Metrics
Alpha Generation 25% 3Y alpha, 5Y alpha, rolling 3Y beat ratio
Risk-Adjusted Returns 25% Sharpe ratio, Sortino ratio, information ratio
Return Consistency 25% Capture spread, rolling consistency
Downside Protection 20% Down-capture ratio (inverted)
Cost Efficiency 5% Expense ratio (inverted percentile)
Output Composite score 0-100 + rating band
Layer 3

Qualitative Analyst Overlay

Numbers catch pattern, but they miss context. The qualitative layer adds a /10 analyst score based on dimensions that purely quantitative screens cannot capture. This overlay can upgrade or downgrade a fund's final rating.

FM Pedigree Manager tenure, track record, team depth
Key-Person Risk H / M / L classification
AUM Capacity Size vs. strategy fit
Style Drift Mandate adherence over time
ER Spread Regular - Direct (distributor fee proxy)
Client Suitability Risk profile match
Findings

What the Data Shows

After running 80 funds through the full scoring engine, several patterns emerged that challenge conventional fund selection logic.

01

Most Funds Destroy Alpha

Only 29% of funds generated positive 3-year alpha over their SEBI-mandated benchmark. Over 5 years, the number drops to 25%. The majority of actively managed equity funds in India are not earning their fees on a risk-adjusted basis.

02

Ratings Skew Heavily Negative

Of 80 scored funds, only 9 earned Strong Buy (11.3%) and 12 earned Buy (15%). Meanwhile, 21 funds scored Avoid and 21 scored Underweight. The framework is deliberately harsh: genuine alpha generators are rare, and the model reflects that.

03

Downside Protection Differentiates

The strongest predictor of top-quartile composite scores was not raw alpha but asymmetric capture ratios. Funds that captured less downside relative to their upside participation consistently ranked higher. Losing less matters more than gaining more.

04

Category Matters for Interpretation

Large-cap funds show the widest score dispersion (15.8 to 92.5), while small-cap funds cluster more tightly. This makes sense: the large-cap benchmark (Nifty 100 TRI) is harder to beat, so genuine outperformers stand out more starkly against the noise.

05

Cost Is Not the Differentiator You Think

Cost Efficiency carries only 5% weight in the model, and for good reason. Among direct plans that already passed the ER gate, the cost spread between funds is narrow. What separates top from bottom is alpha generation and risk management, not a 10bps difference in expense ratios.

06

ER Spread Reveals Distribution Economics

The gap between Regular and Direct plan expense ratios (ER Spread) is a proxy for distributor commissions. Funds with unusually high ER Spreads are spending heavily on distribution, which raises questions about whether AUM growth is organic or fee-driven.

Data

Sources & Limitations

All fund data is sourced from ACE Mutual Fund, which aggregates data from AMC filings with SEBI and AMFI. The dataset contains 47 variables per fund across returns, risk ratios, portfolio composition, rolling returns, and benchmark data. Data is as of the most recent available filing period.

Key limitations: The model uses point-in-time snapshots, not time-series panels. Rolling return data is pre-computed by ACE MF at the 1-month frequency (1Y, 3Y, 5Y, 10Y windows). Alpha is calculated as fund CAGR minus benchmark CAGR, which is an arithmetic approximation, not a regression-based Jensen's alpha. Survivorship bias is partially present since the ACE MF universe only includes currently active schemes.

The model is designed for relative ranking within categories, not absolute return prediction. A "Strong Buy" rating means a fund scores well relative to peers on the chosen factors. It is not investment advice.
